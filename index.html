And so I attempt to explore a doodle dataset from Google's Quick Draw which is the largest published doodle dataset generated as a product of one of its AI experiment.

The attempt here is to analyze these drawings from a human lense. And by doing that, trying to learn how people see their world.

This game challenges players to draw a representation of an object within 20 seconds, while the game's neural network tries to recognize the drawing in real-time. By collecting drawings from people around the world, this AI tries to enhance its ability to understand human-generated visual content.


A Neural Network is a series of algorithms that tries to recognize underlying relationships in a set of data through a process that mimics the way the human brain operates.



The data is not represented as the drawing itself. The drawing for each category is stored in a (dot)NDJSON format as an array of strokes where each stroke is a sequence of x and y coordinates. It represents the vector drawing commands to recreate the drawing.

There are 50 million rows of this data across different categories. There are 345 categories such as apple, circle, yoga, zigzag to name a few. The location of people participation is scattered across over 200 countries. So we have to know where mosst of these drawings are coming from.

United States has the largest number of drawings. Size of enclosing rectangle below represents the drawings from the US. United Kingdom is the second highest with a huge gap.




Methodology: Entires from House, Dishwasher, Fan and Blackberry datasets were summed and distributed by Country. Percent of recognized and unrecognized drawings were calculated.


The data above is skewed. There are also a few other countries apart from the ones shown that have very minimalistic numbers. A few up to 10,000 would be Tunisia, Cyprus, Maldives, Bermuda, but at the extreme bottom were less than 20 observations were possible would be Ethiopia, Mali, Guinea.
