Here I attempt to explore the Google's Quick Draw dataset which is the worlds largest published doodle dataset containing 50 million drawings belonging to different categories such as an axe, a football, a flower, a zigzag and even the Eiffel Tower.

It is a game that challenges players to draw a representation of an object or concept within a limited time frame, typically 20 seconds, while the game's neural network tries to recognize the drawing in real-time. It serves as a research project to improve machine learning technology. By collecting drawings from people around the world, Google's AI learns to recognize a wide variety of doodling styles, shapes, and concepts, enhancing its ability to understand human-generated visual content.

Except that the data is not represented as the doodle itself. The doodle for each category is stored in a (dot)NDJSON format as an array of strokes where each stroke is a sequence of x and y coordinates. It represents the vector drawing commands to recreate the drawing.

Let's take a look at the sample data for a house.


There are 50 million rows of this data across different categories. And these are real people who have drawn them. The location of these people is scattered across over 200 countries.

United States has the largest number of drawings and to represent them, the size of the enclosing rectangle below represents the drawings within the US. United Kingdom the second highest with a huge gap.





The data above is skewed. There are also a few other countries apart from the ones shown that have very minimalistic numbers. A few up to 10,000 would be Tunisia, Cyprus, Maldives, Bermuda, but at the extreme bottom were less than 20 observations were possible would be Ethiopia, Mali, Guinea.

Not every part of every country in the World has equal access to internet and so, as we may see, the data is always skewed positively towards the developed countries.
